\begin{abstract}
Existing measures of chain-of-thought (CoT) faithfulness are behavioural:
they perturb the visible reasoning and watch the answer. We propose
\GRACE{} (\emph{Graph Reasoning Attribution for CoT Evaluation}), a
parameter-free divergence over sparse-autoencoder attribution graphs that
quantifies how a model's internal mechanism reorganises when its CoT is
perturbed. Evaluated on Gemma-2-2B-it across 143 paired CoT items from BBH
and MMLU, $\gdiv$ correlates with Lanham's area-over-completeness at
$\rho = {+}0.44$ (95\% CI $[+0.29, +0.56]$) and improves over textual
baselines with confidence intervals on the gap excluding zero. Decomposing
the composite AOC reveals a dissociation: $\gdiv$ tracks the truncation
half of behavioural faithfulness ($\rho = {+}0.35$) but is uncorrelated
with the mistake-injection half ($\rho = {-}0.09$), with paired-bootstrap
CI on the difference of $[+0.33, +0.76]$. Behavioural CoT faithfulness, as
currently scored, is not a single property but a composite of
mechanistically distinct phenomena.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chain-of-thought is the surface that humans, safety monitors, and
downstream pipelines read when they want to understand what a language
model is doing \citep{lanham2023, turpin2023}. The recurring concern is
that this surface can be unfaithful: the visible reasoning need not
reflect the computation that produced the answer
\citep{barez2025, anthropic-reasoning-2025}. Calibrating how much trust
to place in a model's stated reasoning therefore depends on having a
measurement of faithfulness that is reliable and mechanism-aware.

Existing measurements are predominantly behavioural. Lanham's
area-over-completeness (AOC) truncates or corrupts the CoT and tracks how
the answer changes \citep{lanham2023}; \citet{turpin2023} biases the
prompt with a hint and counts answer flips. Subsequent refinements
\citep{atanasova2023, parcalabescu2024, meek2025} keep the measurement
strictly in output space. Such protocols are necessarily indirect: they
cannot easily separate a model whose answer is supported by a different
mechanism than its CoT suggests from one whose answer is simply robust
to the chosen perturbation.

Sparse-autoencoder attribution graphs offer a more direct vantage point.
For a single forward pass, the \texttt{circuit-tracer} pipeline
\citep{hanna2025} records which Gemma Scope features
\citep{lieberum2024} influenced a target token through which
residual-stream paths. Comparing the graphs produced under a base CoT
and a perturbed counterpart should, in principle, reveal whether the
underlying mechanism reorganised, but quantitative metrics over such
paired graphs have not been proposed.

This paper introduces \GRACE{}, a parameter-free attribution-graph
divergence that operationalises the comparison as a single
influence-weighted Jaccard over the most salient features in each graph.
Evaluated on Gemma-2-2B-it \citep{gemmateam2024} across 143 paired CoT
items spanning BBH and MMLU, $\gdiv$ correlates with Lanham AOC at
$\rho = {+}0.44$, holds independently on each dataset, and outperforms
edit-distance and length baselines with confidence intervals on the gap
that exclude zero. Beyond this aggregate correlation, decomposing the
AOC composite into its constituent perturbation protocols exposes a
dissociation: $\gdiv$ correlates strongly with the truncation
sub-protocols and is uncorrelated with the mistake-injection
sub-protocol. The paired-bootstrap CI on the difference is
$[+0.33, +0.76]$. We read this as evidence that behavioural CoT
faithfulness, as it is currently scored, blends mechanistically distinct
phenomena, and that a single faithfulness number is the wrong
abstraction over the protocols that produce it.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Behavioural faithfulness.}
\citet{lanham2023} introduced AOC, a composite that aggregates four
sub-protocols: early-answering, truncation at $\{25, 50, 75\}\%$ of the
CoT, and mistake injection. \citet{turpin2023} probed faithfulness
through hint injection: prepending a biasing cue to the prompt and
measuring whether the answer flips while the CoT omits the cue. Recent
work has refined such protocols
\citep{atanasova2023, parcalabescu2024, meek2025}, but the underlying
object of measurement remains the model's output behaviour.

\paragraph{Attribution graphs over transcoder features.}
\citet{hanna2025} released \texttt{circuit-tracer}, which constructs
pruned attribution graphs over Gemma Scope sparse-autoencoder features
\citep{lieberum2024}. Each node carries a (layer, position, feature)
identifier; each edge is an influence on a downstream node's
residual-stream contribution. \citet{lindsey2025bio} use such graphs
for qualitative case studies of unfaithful reasoning. To our knowledge,
quantitative metrics over them have not been proposed.

\paragraph{Counterfactual paired comparison versus correctness
classification.}
Concurrent work by \citet{zhao2025crv} also derives signal from
attribution graphs, but along an axis orthogonal to ours. Their CRV
system trains a classifier over single-graph features to predict CoT
\emph{correctness}, that is, whether a given chain of reasoning is
right or wrong, in isolation. Our object of study is faithfulness, not
correctness, and our measurement is counterfactual rather than
classification-based: for each item we construct a paired-graph
experiment in which both members of the pair are produced from the same
prompt but with different CoT conditions, and we score the mechanism
shift between them. Holding the prompt, model, and target token
constant across the pair isolates the contribution of the CoT itself,
so the divergence between the two graphs measures how much the visible
reasoning, as opposed to the rest of the input, drove the answer.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $G(x, c, y)$ denote the pruned attribution graph produced by
\texttt{circuit-tracer} for input $x$, model-generated CoT $c$, and
answer token $y$. A \emph{paired-graph experiment} is a tuple
$\mathcal{E} = (x, c, c', y)$ where $c'$ is a perturbation of $c$. We
compute $G_0 = G(x, c, y)$ and $G_1 = G(x, c', y)$, both over the same
shared feature space. \GRACE{} is the divergence between $G_0$ and
$G_1$.

\paragraph{Concept-level features.}
Nodes in $G$ carry identifiers of the form $(\ell, p, f)$ for layer
$\ell$, token position $p$, and feature index $f$. A naive Jaccard over
these tuples silently measures sequence-length drift: if CoT$_0$ is 30
tokens and CoT$_1$ is 20 tokens, they will occupy different position ranges,
so the same conceptual feature at different positions registers as entirely
different nodes, artificially inflating divergence. To isolate mechanism
reorganisation from sequence-length effects, we strip position and define
the \emph{concept} of a node as
\[
  \concept(n) = (\ell, f),
\]
collapsing all positional copies of the same Gemma Scope feature
within the same layer into a single concept. Each concept inherits an
influence weight by max-pooling absolute attribution to the answer
token across positions:
$w(\concept) = \max_{n : \phi(n) = \concept}\, |w(n)|$. The
concept-level representation is the unit over which the divergence is
defined.

\paragraph{The metric.}
Let $\mathcal{N}_k(G)$ denote the top-$k$ concepts in $G$ ranked by
$w(\concept)$. \GRACE{} is one minus the influence-weighted Jaccard
over these sets:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:grace}
\gdiv(G_0, G_1) =
1 -
\frac{
  \sum_{\concept \in \mathcal{N}_k(G_0) \cap \mathcal{N}_k(G_1)}
       \min(w_0(\concept), w_1(\concept))
}{
  \sum_{\concept \in \mathcal{N}_k(G_0) \cup \mathcal{N}_k(G_1)}
       \max(w_0(\concept), w_1(\concept))
}.
\end{equation}
$\gdiv \in [0, 1]$; low values indicate that the same influential
concepts carry similar weight in both graphs, so the mechanism is
largely intact, and values approaching one indicate that the
influential concepts have reorganised. We use $k = 64$ throughout the
main text and ablate $k \in \{16, 32, 64, 128\}$ in
\Cref{app:k}; the metric has no other parameters.
\Cref{fig:pipeline} summarises the end-to-end measurement: paired CoT
conditions yield paired attribution graphs over the answer token, nodes
are projected to concepts, and the influence-weighted Jaccard over the
top-$k$ concepts gives $\gdiv$.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/f1_method}
  \caption{The \GRACE{} measurement pipeline. Two CoT conditions on the
           same prompt yield paired attribution graphs over the answer
           token. Nodes are aggregated to concept-level features by
           collapsing positional copies within a layer. The
           influence-weighted Jaccard over the top-$k$ concepts produces
           $\gdiv \in [0, 1]$.}
  \label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Three paired regimes.}
We construct three paired-perturbation regimes (\Cref{tab:regimes}).
Regime~\textbf{A} is paraphrase invariance, in which $c'$ is a
same-answer paraphrase of $c$, so $\gdiv$ is expected to be low and the
regime serves as a sanity check. Regime~\textbf{B} is Lanham-style
truncation or mistake injection on the CoT, where $\gdiv$ is expected
to track behavioural AOC. Regime~\textbf{C} is Turpin-style hint
injection, which alters the prompt rather than the CoT and is included
as a scope check; we report it in \Cref{app:hint}.

\label{tab:regimes}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\paragraph{Model and graphs.}
All experiments use Gemma-2-2B-it (fp16) \citep{gemmateam2024} with the
full 26-layer Gemma Scope per-layer-transcoder stack. Attribution
graphs are produced by \texttt{circuit-tracer} \citep{hanna2025}; we
generated approximately 780 graphs in three to four hours on a single
A100, covering the paired-perturbation conditions described in
\Cref{sec:method}.

\paragraph{Data.}
Items are drawn from BBH \citep{suzgun2022}, MMLU
\citep{hendrycks2020mmlu}, GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021gsm8k}, and the Turpin
hint set \citep{turpin2023}. After AOC labelling, $143$ items have
valid Regime-B paired graphs and behavioural ground truth ($77$ from
BBH, $66$ from MMLU); $287$ pairs are available for Regime~C. All
reported numbers are computed on the full set of valid items.

\paragraph{Research questions.}
We evaluate \GRACE{} on three questions. The first is whether $\gdiv$
correlates with Lanham AOC, the most established behavioural measure
of CoT faithfulness. The second is whether it improves over textual
baselines that any same-prompt perturbation must introduce. The third
is whether, on decomposing the AOC composite into its constituent
perturbation sub-protocols, $\gdiv$ correlates uniformly across them
or differentially.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare \GRACE{} against four baselines that do not require a
transcoder stack: normalised edit distance over CoT tokens
(\texttt{edit\_norm}), absolute CoT length difference in tokens
(\texttt{len\_diff}), graph-size differences ($|N_0| - |N_1|$), and an
unweighted shared-concept fraction that uses the same concept-level
representation as \GRACE{} but discards the influence weights.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Behavioural-faithfulness correlation.}
On the 143-item Regime-B set, $\rho(\gdiv, \text{AOC}) = {+}0.444$,
$p \ll 10^{-3}$, with BCa 95\% CI $[+0.29, +0.56]$. The relationship
holds independently on each dataset (BBH: $\rho = {+}0.42$, $n{=}77$;
MMLU: $\rho = {+}0.49$, $n{=}66$). \Cref{fig:scatter} shows the
per-item scatter of $\gdiv$ against AOC.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/f2_correlation}
  \caption{Per-item mean $\gdiv$ versus Lanham AOC composite on the
           143-item Regime-B set. Each point is one item; the orange
           line is an OLS fit shown for visualisation. Spearman
           $\rho = {+}0.44$, BCa 95\% CI $[+0.29, +0.56]$. Items with
           higher $\gdiv$ exhibit higher AOC, indicating that the
           mechanism reorganises more when the CoT is perturbed for
           items whose answer is more behaviourally sensitive to that
           perturbation.}
  \label{fig:scatter}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Strict improvement over textual baselines.}
\GRACE{} outperforms each textual baseline by a margin whose
paired-bootstrap confidence interval excludes zero. Against normalised
edit distance, $\Delta\rho = {+}0.329$, 95\% CI $[+0.080, +0.578]$.
Against length difference, $\Delta\rho = {+}0.336$, 95\% CI
$[+0.091, +0.561]$. Partial Spearman of $\gdiv$ against AOC,
controlling jointly for \texttt{edit\_norm}, \texttt{len\_diff}, and
graph-size baselines, is $\rho = {+}0.341$, $p < 10^{-4}$. The signal
is therefore not driven by the trivial textual or structural
confounders that any same-prompt perturbation introduces.

\paragraph{Mechanistic dissociation.}
Decomposing the AOC composite per perturbation sub-protocol exposes a
sharp asymmetry (\Cref{tab:dissociation}). $\gdiv$ correlates with each
of the three truncation sub-scores at $\rho \in [{+}0.30, {+}0.35]$
($p < 10^{-3}$), and is uncorrelated with the mistake-injection
sub-score ($\rho = {-}0.09$, $p = 0.28$). The paired-bootstrap CI on
the difference of correlations,
$\rho(\gdiv, \text{AOC}_{\text{trunc}}) -
 \rho(\gdiv, \text{AOC}_{\text{mistake}})$,
is $[+0.33, +0.76]$ around a point estimate of ${+}0.537$.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering\small
\caption{Per-component correlation of $\gdiv$ with AOC sub-scores on
         the full 143-item set. Truncation sub-scores correlate
         strongly; mistake injection is uncorrelated.}
\label{tab:dissociation}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
AOC component & $\rho(\gdiv, \cdot)$ & $p$ \\
\midrule
\texttt{aoc\_truncate\_25}     & ${+}0.300$ & $3{\times}10^{-4}$ \\
\texttt{aoc\_truncate\_50}     & ${+}0.348$ & ${<}10^{-4}$ \\
\texttt{aoc\_truncate\_75}     & ${+}0.343$ & ${<}10^{-4}$ \\
\texttt{aoc\_mistake}          & ${-}0.091$ & $0.28$ \\
\midrule
\texttt{aoc\_composite}        & ${+}0.444$ & ${\ll}10^{-3}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The same metric, computed over the same items, correlates with one
half of the AOC composite and is uncorrelated with the other half. We
interpret this in mechanistic terms in \Cref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Ablations.}
\textit{Influence weighting.} Replacing the influence-weighted Jaccard
in \Cref{eq:grace} with the unweighted shared-concept fraction reduces
$\rho$ from ${+}0.444$ to ${+}0.284$, a $\Delta\rho$ of ${-}0.16$, so
the weighting contributes a substantial fraction of the correlation
with AOC. \textit{Top-$k$ robustness.} Across
$k \in \{16, 32, 64, 128\}$, $\rho$ ranges over $[{+}0.34, {+}0.37]$,
with all four values significant at $p < 10^{-3}$
(\Cref{app:k}). The default $k = 64$ is not a tuned operating point.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\GRACE{} reads CoT faithfulness directly from the model's own
attribution graph, summarising the comparison between paired CoT
conditions in a single influence-weighted Jaccard. On 143 paired items
from BBH and MMLU it correlates with Lanham AOC at
$\rho = {+}0.44$, beats edit-distance and length baselines with
confidence intervals that exclude zero, and reveals an asymmetry
inside the AOC composite that behavioural composites are not designed
to surface.

\paragraph{Behavioural CoT faithfulness is not a single property.}
The dissociation in \Cref{tab:dissociation} is the paper's main
scientific finding. The AOC composite blends two distinct perturbation
protocols. Truncation perturbs the CoT by removing its tail and asks
whether the model still arrives at the same answer; this is a
measurement of \emph{CoT necessity}, the degree to which the model's
answer depends on the CoT actually being executed. Mistake injection
corrupts the CoT by inserting a wrong intermediate step and asks
whether the model still arrives at the correct answer; this is a
measurement of \emph{error detection}, the degree to which the model
can recognise and discount a faulty step in its own reasoning. Both
are reasonable operationalisations of CoT faithfulness, and current
practice aggregates them into a single AOC score.

\GRACE{} detects mechanism reorganisation between paired graphs. It
correlates with truncation sub-scores because removing the tail of
the CoT, when the CoT was load-bearing, reorganises which concepts
dominate the influence onto the answer token. It does not correlate
with mistake injection because inserting a single wrong step into an
otherwise unchanged CoT need not reorganise the mechanism: the model
may carry the error forward through the same concepts that produced
the original answer, or detect and discount it through localised
features that a top-$k$ summary of the influence-weighted graph does
not foreground. Truncation and mistake injection probe mechanistically
distinct phenomena, and a metric that measures global mechanism
reorganisation is the right instrument for one and the wrong
instrument for the other.

The implication for benchmark design is that aggregating sub-protocols
into a single CoT-faithfulness score loses information about the
mechanism that produced the score. Reporting the truncation and
mistake-injection axes separately, and pairing each with a
mechanism-level metric appropriate to it, gives a more discriminating
picture of when a model is actually using its CoT.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
We evaluate one model (Gemma-2-2B-it) with one transcoder family
(Gemma Scope per-layer transcoders); the metric inherits the
limitations of its dictionary, and cross-model evaluation requires a
model-matched transcoder family. $\gdiv$ is correlational, and we have
not run causal interventions on the concepts it flags as divergent.
Pruned attribution graphs are themselves a summary of the underlying
computation, not a complete record.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Three directions are open. The first is a complementary mechanism-level
metric for the error-detection axis, since the dissociation implies
one is needed. Error-correction features that activate locally around
inserted mistakes, visible in the attribution graph but not foregrounded
by a global top-$k$ summary, are a natural starting point. The second
is causal feature ablation on the concepts $\gdiv$ flags as divergent:
if the metric correctly identifies the mechanism that carried the
answer, ablating those concepts should reproduce the perturbation's
behavioural effect. The third is cross-model evaluation as transcoder
dictionaries become available for additional architectures.

\section{Influence-weighting ablation}
\label{app:influence}

Replacing the influence-weighted Jaccard $\Jw$ in \Cref{eq:grace} with
the unweighted shared-concept fraction
$|\mathcal{N}_k(G_0) \cap \mathcal{N}_k(G_1)| /
 |\mathcal{N}_k(G_0) \cup \mathcal{N}_k(G_1)|$
reduces $\rho(\cdot, \text{AOC}_{\text{composite}})$ from
${+}0.444$ (95\% CI $[+0.29, +0.56]$) to ${+}0.284$
(95\% CI $[+0.13, +0.42]$). Treating all top-$k$ concepts as equally
important therefore costs roughly a third of the metric's correlation
with AOC, so the influence-weighting is retained throughout the paper.

\section{Per-regime breakdown of the headline correlation}
\label{app:regime}

The Regime-B set decomposes into two perturbation types. The headline
correlation holds in both, with truncation pairs driving the bulk of
the signal:
\begin{itemize}
  \setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}\setlength{\topsep}{2pt}
  \item Truncation pairs only: $\rho = {+}0.402$, 95\% CI
        $[+0.25, +0.53]$.
  \item Mistake-injection pairs only: $\rho = {+}0.223$, 95\% CI
        $[+0.04, +0.38]$.
\end{itemize}
This regime-level pattern is the same effect reported per-AOC-component
in \Cref{tab:dissociation}, viewed at the level of the perturbation
that produced each pair rather than the AOC sub-score that scored it.

\section{Hint-injection regime as scope check}
\label{app:hint}

Regime~C consists of Turpin-style hint-injection pairs, in which the
prompt rather than the CoT is perturbed. As a scope check, we evaluated
whether $\gdiv$, computed over the answer-token attribution graphs of
the unbiased and biased prompts, predicts the binary outcome of whether
the model's answer flips toward the hint. On 287 pairs, $\gdiv$
achieves AUROC $= 0.47$, 95\% CI $[0.40, 0.54]$, indistinguishable from
chance. Because Regime~C perturbs the prompt rather than the CoT, the
two attribution graphs diverge for reasons unrelated to the CoT
mechanism that \GRACE{} is designed to summarise; this regime is
included for completeness rather than as a positive result.

\section{Neuronpedia interpretations of high-influence concepts}
\label{app:neuronpedia}

We inspected the most influential concepts in Regime-B graphs by
querying their Neuronpedia entries. Concepts that appear as
high-influence in over $80\%$ of pairs (i.e. domain-general backbone
features) include \texttt{L22\_F11133} (``narrative progression''),
\texttt{L24\_F12351} (``political positions''), and
\texttt{L25\_F5714} (``small sample sizes in studies''). Concepts that
gain influence specifically in high-$\gdiv$ pairs (i.e. that appear
when the CoT is structurally engaged) include \texttt{L24\_F6157}
(``time'') and \texttt{L23\_F10308} (``numerical data patterns''). The
qualitative shift from generic backbone concepts to item-specific
concepts under high-$\gdiv$ conditions is consistent with the headline
correlation: when the CoT is genuinely load-bearing, the model routes
through domain-specific features, and an aggregate metric registers
the shift as a mechanism reorganisation.
